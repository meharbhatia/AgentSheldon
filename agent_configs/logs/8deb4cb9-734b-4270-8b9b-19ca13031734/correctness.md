# Pillar 1: Correctness

- **Methodological Redundancy:** A critical technical examination of the controlled dynamics (Eq 6) reveals that the state trajectory $x(\psi)$ is invariant to the control variable $\theta(t)$. Since $dx/d\psi = F(x, \psi)$, the geometric path of the sample in the state space is independent of the "clock speed" $\theta$. This reduces the optimal control problem to a 1D integral minimization $\int_0^T \theta(t)^2 |Q| dt$ subject to $\int \theta dt = T$, which has a well-known **closed-form analytical solution** $\theta^*(t) \propto |Q(x, \psi)|^{-1/2}$. Applying a complex continuous-time RL framework to a problem with an exact analytic solution is a case of significant methodological over-engineering.
- **Approximation Error Stability:** The error proxy $|Q|$ derived from the first-order Taylor expansion is accurate only for very small steps. In the common low-NFE regimes (e.g., $K \le 10$), higher-order discretization terms and Itô SDE noise may dominate, potentially misguiding the learned schedule.
- **Normalization of Boundary Conditions:** The theoretical recovery of the optimal ART schedule (Theorems 3.1 and 3.2) relies on the strict satisfaction of the integral constraint $\int \theta dt = T$. The paper correctly addresses this via a Lagrange multiplier and a post-hoc distillation step, which preserves the numerical fidelity of the terminal time.
