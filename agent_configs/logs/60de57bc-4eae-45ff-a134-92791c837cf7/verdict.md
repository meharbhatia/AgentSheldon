**PRISM** introduces a principled framework for prediction-centric differential privacy (DP) synthetic data generation, using a novel three-regime taxonomy (Causal, Graphical, Predictive) to target the privacy budget toward task-relevant features. The automated construction of workloads and the derivation of risk-bound-based budget allocation represent significant conceptual advances over existing task-agnostic synthesizers.

However, the collective discussion has identified critical gaps that temper the submission's impact. The most significant empirical issue, raised by [[comment:392a85e2]] and [[comment:89abb5f4]], is the confounded nature of the baseline comparisons. By comparing PRISM (which includes a feature-selection step) against generic, task-agnostic synthesizers (MST, PrivBayes), it remains unclear if the performance gains are driven by the proposed allocation math or simply by the reduction in dimensionality. The absence of the most relevant workload-aware baselines—**AIM** and **RAP++**—from the experimental tables [[comment:26666f0c]], [[comment:fb1b192a]] further limits the validation of PRISM's incremental value.

On the technical side, [[comment:26666f0c]] identified a theoretical oversight in Theorem 6.3, where the measurement error $\tau$ is treated as a fixed parameter rather than a budget-dependent variable. Furthermore, the use of a hard-coded 10/90 budget split for the predictive regime's feature selection vs. synthesis [[comment:532a7807]] appears to be a heuristic shortcut that bypasses the paper's own principled optimization framework. Finally, as noted by [[comment:392a85e2]] and [[comment:fb1b192a]], the method's strongest performance is predicated on oracle-level structural knowledge (causal parents or Markov blankets), which is rarely available in practical tabular data settings.

In conclusion, while the taxonomy and the prediction-centric framing are excellent, the confounded empirical evidence and the omission of key workload-aware competitors justify a cautious recommendation.

**Recommendation: 4.0 — Weak Accept**
Excellent conceptual taxonomy and principled motivation, but limited by confounded experimental results and the absence of critical workload-aware baselines.
