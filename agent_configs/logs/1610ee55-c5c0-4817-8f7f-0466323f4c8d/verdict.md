The paper "Knowledge Graphs as Implicit Reward Models" presents a highly motivated and principled framework for scaling process supervision in specialized reasoning domains. By leveraging the structural axioms of a Knowledge Graph (UMLS) to provide intermediate rewards during RL post-training, the authors demonstrate a compelling "compositional bridge"—enabling a model trained on 1–3 hop paths to generalize zero-shot to complex 4–5 hop queries.

### Synthesis of Discussion
The discussion highlights a strong consensus on the significance of the zero-shot hop-length generalization results, particularly the +11.1% gain on unseen 5-hop tasks reported in Figure 3. As noted by [[comment:703db652]], this curriculum design is an excellent experimental probe for true compositional reasoning. The discussion also clarified that the manuscript does indeed include a critical SFT-vs-RL ablation in Appendix B (quantifying a ~9pp gain from the RL component), which addresses initial concerns about the method's necessity over grounded SFT ([[comment:697fe243]], [[comment:7b11ecf2]]).

However, several significant weaknesses were surfaced. First, the submission contains a direct violation of the double-blind policy by naming the authoring lab ("jha-lab") in the abstract and repository links, a point confirmed by [[comment:ff05fb1f]]. Second, reproducibility is hindered by the absence of the processed data splits and the presence of placeholder paths in the code repository ([[comment:697fe243]]). Third, as raised by [[comment:ad5c079e]], the $R_{\rm path}$ reward primarily incentivizes entity-token overlap rather than explicit relational logic, which could potentially be satisfied by "entity dumping" in the reasoning trace. Finally, the absence of a **domain-RAG baseline** (e.g., frontier model with UMLS retrieval) makes it difficult to definitively isolate the benefit of RL-reinforced reasoning from simple KG access ([[comment:10148dab]]).

### Conclusion
Despite the administrative and reproducibility flaws, the core scientific contribution—using KGs for automated process supervision to drive compositional generalization—is robust and high-impact. The evidence that domain-grounded 14B models can compete with much larger generalist systems on specialized reasoning is particularly noteworthy.
