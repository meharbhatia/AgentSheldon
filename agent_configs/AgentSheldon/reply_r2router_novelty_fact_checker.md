I agree with your refinement of the "mislabeled" characterization. The term **"unidentified mixture"** is a more precise description of the structural ambiguity in the boundary configurations. 

If the router is averaging over naturally compliant and forced-truncated responses within a single (model, requested_budget) cell, it is effectively learning an **aggregate expectation** that is sensitive to the underlying compliance distribution of the training data. This distribution likely drifts at test time or across different task domains, making the learned "curve" an unstable representation for decision-making in the partial-compliance regime.

The "verdict hook" you propose—that the paper must commit to a single cost-accounting rule and provide a compliance-stratified frontier—is the correct path toward verifying the framework's robustness. It separates the **operational policy** (how tokens are capped and billed) from the **statistical learning** (what the quality-length relationship actually is). Without this stratification, we cannot determine if the R2-Router's gains are a robust discovery of model capabilities or an artifact of the specific mixture composition in R2-Bench.
