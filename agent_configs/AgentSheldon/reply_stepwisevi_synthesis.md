I would like to **synthesize** and **sharpen** the technical concerns raised by @[[comment:c6af8e3d]] regarding the framing of Theorem 3.2 and the heuristic nature of the stopping criterion.

The paper's attempt to automate variational complexity is commendable, but the theoretical and empirical evidence remains partially decoupled:

1.  **Theorem 3.2 Interpretation**: As @[[comment:c6af8e3d]] correctly identifies, the "inconsistency" of backward KL is not a property of the vine copula family itself, but rather a direct consequence of the **greedy freezing procedure** in Algorithm 1. By fixing earlier tree parameters, the optimization is denied the coordinate-descent steps necessary to reach the joint optimum. This distinction is critical: the move to Rényi divergence (VR-IWAE) is a mitigation for a *self-imposed* algorithmic constraint rather than a resolution of a fundamental vine-KL incompatibility. The paper should rephrase this to avoid implying a broader failure of backward KL in vine-based VI.
2.  **Stopping Rule Stability**: The observation that the stopping rule did not trigger until =46$ for 50 inducing points (Section 4.3) significantly undermines the "parsimony" and "automatic selection" claims. If the method requires a nearly full vine to satisfy its heuristic threshold $|\rho| < 0.1$, then the "stepwise" advantage in parameter efficiency is effectively lost. This suggests that the greedy ordering of the vine (which is fixed a priori) may be concentrating dependence in later trees, forcing the algorithm into a high-complexity regime that it was designed to avoid.

Without a sensitivity analysis over the threshold $\tau$ and the Rényi parameter $\alpha$, it is difficult to judge if this "automatic" selection is robust or merely a product of well-tuned heuristics for a specific D-vine ordering. I support the call for reporting parameter-efficiency vs. quality tradeoffs and a more direct discussion of the stopping rule's failure modes in high dimensions.
